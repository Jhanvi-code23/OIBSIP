BIOGRAPHY

Leonardo da Vinci was more than a painter—he was a visionary whose curiosity reshaped the boundaries of human knowledge. Born in 1452 in the small village of Vinci, Italy, he spent his life asking questions that few people of his time dared to imagine. Whether observing the flight of birds or studying the intricate structure of the human body, Leonardo believed that every detail of nature held a lesson waiting to be discovered.

His journey took him through the artistic centers of Florence and Milan, where he created masterpieces that continue to inspire the world centuries later. Yet, his genius was never confined to a single discipline. While the world admired his paintings, Leonardo quietly filled notebooks with ideas for flying machines, engineering marvels, anatomical discoveries, and scientific observations that were far ahead of their era.

What makes Leonardo da Vinci truly remarkable is not only the brilliance of his creations but also the spirit with which he pursued knowledge. He viewed art and science as two sides of the same coin, proving that creativity flourishes when imagination and observation work together. His relentless curiosity transformed ordinary questions into extraordinary discoveries.

Even today, Leonardo's legacy continues to inspire artists, scientists, inventors, engineers, and dreamers around the world. His life reminds us that greatness is not measured by a single achievement but by an endless desire to learn, explore, and leave the world richer than we found it.

JOURNEY THROUGH TIME

Birth in Vinci

1452

Born in the small village of Vinci, Italy, Leonardo would go on to become one of history's most influential figures.

Apprenticeship

1466

Began his artistic training under the renowned painter Andrea del Verrocchio, where he honed his skills and developed his unique style.

Guild of St. Luke

1472

Leonardo officially became a member of Florence's Guild of Saint Luke, allowing him to establish himself as an independent artist.

Journey to Milan

1482

Leonardo moved to Milan to serve Duke Ludovico Sforza, beginning one of the most productive periods of his career as an artist and engineer.

Virgin of the Rocks

1483–1486

He completed one of his earliest masterpieces, admired for its atmospheric perspective and graceful composition.

Vitruvian Man

1490

Leonardo combined art, anatomy, and mathematics to create the iconic drawing that symbolizes human proportion and balance.

The Last Supper

1495–1498

One of the greatest religious paintings ever created, celebrated for its emotional storytelling and revolutionary composition.

A New Beginning

1499

Following the French invasion of Milan, Leonardo left the city and continued his artistic and scientific pursuits elsewhere in Italy.

Mona Lisa

1503-1506

Leonardo began painting the Mona Lisa, whose mysterious smile has captivated millions for over five centuries.

Anatomical Studies

1508-1513

Leonardo conducted extensive anatomical studies, dissecting cadavers to understand the human body and incorporating this knowledge into his artistic work.

Life in France

1516

King Francis I invited Leonardo to France, where he spent his final years continuing to write, sketch, and mentor others.

An Eternal Legacy

1519

Leonardo passed away on 2 May 1519, leaving behind a legacy that continues to inspire artists, scientists, inventors, and dreamers around the world.
